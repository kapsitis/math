This was actually in the contest, wasn't it?

It's kind of hard to put into words. The red points consists of a series of columns of decreasing height (I mean decreasing starting from left to right, and I don't mean strictly decreasing). The blue points consist of the completion of the red columns up to the border of the triangle having vertices . At the same time, the blue and red sets can also be regarded as rows instead of columns.

is simply the product of the lengths of all blue columns, while is the product of the lengths of all blue rows. If we show that the same numbers, with the same multiplicities, appear in the collections of a) blue column lengths and b) blue row lengths, then we are clearly done. Call this assertion .

The blue set can be regarded as the union of some maximal right triangles with the legs parallel to the axes of the plane (maximal in the sense that they aren't part of any other similar right triangles). The triangles are obtained as follows: from a point on the diagonal go down as far as you can without taking any red points. Now go to the right until you meet the diagonal again. The maximal triangles of this set are the ones we're looking for.

We can now prove the assertion by induction on .

If there are points on the diagonal which are red, then it's easy to see that the number of blue columns of length is equal to the number of blue rows of length , and we can look at the picture as the union of some smaller pictures (for smaller 's), by breaking the diagonal where it has red points. We use the induction hypothesis on these smaller pictures.

On the other hand, if no points on are red, then we can look at the points strictly below the diagonal , and use the induction hypothesis on these. After we append the diagonal all lengths (of blue columns and rows) increase by , so we have shown what we wanted: the number of blue columns of length is the same as the number of blue rows of length for all .

Watch out: I might have reversed "red" and "blue" in some places.
